Nobody on the Augusta National leaderboard last week was playing a Cleveland driver. I can say that without checking. The sponsorship machinery doesn't work that way. But here's what that information is worth to you, playing a Saturday morning round at your local muni — nothing. The gear on a Tour leaderboard tells you what elite professionals with custom shaft builds and weekly fittings prefer. It tells you almost nothing about what will help a 15-handicapper keep the ball in play off the first tee.

The Cleveland Launcher XL 2 is a driver built for people who actually buy their own equipment.

## The Club

The head is 460cc — full titanium, maximum legal volume, which means every cubic centimeter of forgiveness the rules permit is sitting in this thing at address. The face uses what Cleveland calls Rebound Frame technology: variable-thickness panels that flex and snap back through impact, storing and releasing energy more efficiently than a conventional face. You have seen this philosophy in Cleveland's wedges for years. The grooves and face flex work there. The same logic applies here, just at driver speeds.

The crown is lightweight carbon. That exists for a single reason — moving weight away from the top of the head pushes the centre of gravity lower and further back. Lower CG means higher launch. Cleveland pairs that with the HiBore crown shape, which is designed to get the ball airborne without demanding you generate your own spin. If you have a slower swing or tend to deloft through impact, that matters more than any marketing claim on the box.

Stock shaft is the Miyazaki C. Kua 40 graphite, available in regular and senior flex, with a Project X Cypher option at stiff. These are not premium aftermarket shafts. They are functional. If you have ever had a fitting and know your launch and spin numbers, swap to something that suits you. If you have not, the stock shaft will not hold you back.

## What the Internet Is Saying

The general verdict from golfers who bought this and played it is: it goes. Distance for the money is the most common compliment. Forgiveness is the second — off-centre strikes hold their line better than you would expect from a driver at this price. The complaint that shows up most consistently is the sound. A few players describe it as hollow. Not unpleasant, just not the solid crack you get from a Titleist TSR or a premium Ping. Chip's take: if the sound of your driver is the primary consideration, you are optimising for the wrong variable.

Nobody is complaining about performance on the course. The complaints — and there are not many — are peripheral. The stock grip is unremarkable. The adjustment wrench is small enough to lose in your bag pocket. A couple of buyers wanted more shaft weight options at purchase. Fair enough. None of that changes what happens when you put it on a tee.

## Chip's Verdict

This is a forgiveness driver priced like a forgiveness driver. Cleveland does not have Callaway's marketing spend or a roster of Tour winners to put in the TV ads. What they have is a club that performs closer to its category ceiling than the price would suggest.

A bloke I caddied for years back never bought a club based on what the Tour was using. He bought what kept him out of the trees and out of trouble, and he played good, honest golf his whole life with equipment nobody at the range recognized. He would have liked this driver. It launches, it carries, and on the off-centre strikes that every amateur hits dozens of times per round, it holds together.

It is not a driver for a low-handicapper trying to work the ball. The high-launch bias and rear CG placement will fight anyone trying to hit a controlled cut under pressure. Cleveland did not design this for that player, and they have never pretended otherwise. If you are mid-to-high handicap, hitting it left and short more than you want to, and not looking to spend four hundred pounds on a driver this spring — this is the honest choice.
