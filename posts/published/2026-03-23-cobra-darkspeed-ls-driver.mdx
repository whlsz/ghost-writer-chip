The bag came in off the range last Tuesday with a DARKSPEED LS on it. The member — a two handicap who changes equipment the way most people change socks — dropped it against the fence and said, without any particular enthusiasm, "It's good." For a two handicap, that's high praise.

I had already been watching the DARKSPEED LS turn up on bags for most of the past season, so his assessment didn't surprise me. What surprised me was the number of mid-handicappers I saw swinging one. This is, by design and by spec, not a forgiving driver. It is a driver for players who know what they're doing with their transition and do not need the club to bail them out.

That distinction matters, because the golf internet tends to lump all distance drivers together, and the Cobra DARKSPEED LS is not the same club as its siblings in the lineup.

The LS stands for low spin. The head is 460cc — full size — but Cobra has shaped it with a lifted rear section and what they call aerodynamic profiling borrowed from aviation and high-performance vehicles. The claim is reduced drag through the swing for higher club head speeds. In practice, it does feel like the head moves through the hitting area cleanly. There is no sense of the head fighting the air, which you sometimes get with deeper-shaped drivers. The matte black finish runs across the entire head — crown, sole, headcover — and the three removable sole weights give you options: push weight toward the heel and toe for face stability, or drop the rear weight in for a bit more draw bias.

The adjustable hosel runs eight loft positions. The stock shaft is a HZRDUS Black Gen 4, which has a stiff butt section, extra-stiff mid, and a stiff tip — suited for players who attack the ball hard and want to feel stability rather than kick. It is not a shaft for everyone. If your tempo is slow and you need the shaft to do work for you, this is the wrong club. If you swing with pace and your miss is a high spin fade, the HZRDUS in this head is a reasonable place to start.

At impact, the DARKSPEED LS is loud. A clean strike delivers a sound that tour players would call crisp and most amateurs would describe as satisfying. A missed strike gives you oscillation and a reminder — which is as it should be for a low-spin driver. The face tells you the truth.

Most reviewers who tested this in 2024 noted that spin rates were genuinely low compared to comparable models from Titleist and TaylorMade, while ball speed numbers held up. The consensus was that this driver delivered more distance for players who already produce enough club head speed to use a low-spin head, but punished slower swingers who relied on spin to keep the ball in the air. Chip's take: they are right. If you are averaging 90 miles per hour on a good day, this is not your driver. If you are regularly at 105 or above and your typical miss spins off to the right, it is worth a serious look.
