Every golfer who watches Augusta starts thinking about their putting. Not their driving. Not their wedges. Their putting. The greens at Augusta National end more rounds than any bunker on the property, and the players who win there are not the ones who drove it longest. They are the ones who read the break on 12 and trusted it. That is the context in which I want to talk about the Cobra Grandsport-35.

Cobra built this putter using HP's Multi Jet Fusion process — industrial 3D printing, not the kind your nephew uses to make figurines. The head is a high-density polymer printed in a lattice structure internally, which sounds like marketing until you understand what it buys you. A CNC machine removes material to achieve weight distribution. A 3D printer places it. The distinction matters for a mallet, where moment of inertia is the entire design objective. Cobra can put mass where the calculation says it belongs, not where the toolpath allows it. On paper — and in practice — that produces a cleaner result than what traditional milling can achieve at this price.

The Grandsport-35 is a full mallet head, face-balanced. The face itself is a separate machined aluminum insert sitting behind a soft elastomer layer, which absorbs impact and quiets the sound at contact. Face-balanced means the toe doesn't drop when you rest it flat on your hand — it sits level, which tells you it's designed for a straight-back-straight-through stroke. If you're an arc putter, this head is not your friend. If your stroke travels on a relatively straight path, it will feel like the putter was made for you.

Most golfers have never been properly matched to a putter head shape. They bought what looked right at the shop, or inherited a blade from someone who wasn't using it, or kept rolling whatever came in their starter set. Face-balanced or arc — it's the single most important axis the putter decision turns on, and most people buying putters never ask the question. If you can't identify your stroke, stand over the ball, rest the putter flat on your hand, and watch whether the toe drops. If it stays level, your stroke probably suits a face-balanced head like this one. If it falls, it doesn't, and you should be looking at something with more toe hang. Know that before you spend money on anything.

Most of the reviewers who spent time with the 3DP line noticed that the feel was softer than expected. The historical knock on polymer-headed putters is that they feel hollow — there is a plasticky quality at impact that better players notice immediately. Cobra's elastomer layer addresses this. It is not the same feel as a milled steel blade. It is softer, more dampened, quieter. There is less feedback in the classical sense. What you gain is roll: the ball comes off the face without drama and tracks its line. Whether that trade is worth making is a personal question.
