The bloke I caddied for at Muirfield in '09 had a fairway wood problem. Not the club itself — the thinking around it. He treated it like a driver from the fairway instead of what it actually is: the club that keeps you out of trouble when the driver already got you into some. I've thought about that every time I see a golfer reach for a new fairway wood expecting miracles. The Cobra DARKSPEED X doesn't do miracles. What it does, at $199, is earn its keep.

The DARKSPEED X is the middle child in Cobra's 2024 fairway wood family — between the MAX, which tilts toward forgiveness, and the LS, which is built for better players wanting lower spin and more workability. The X aims at the middle: neutral ball flight, adjustable settings, enough forgiveness to be useful, not so much that the numbers lie to you. It comes in 3-wood (15°), 5-wood (18°), and 7-wood (21°) configurations. The head sits square at address — not the slightly closed setup you've seen from older Cobra fairway woods, which some players found pushed them left. They fixed that, and the fix is noticeable.

The tech they're most proud of is the aerodynamic shaping and the removable rear weight that lets you shift the center of gravity. Combined with an adjustable hosel giving you roughly one and a half degrees in either direction, there's real room to dial this in. The stock shaft is the UST Mamiya LIN-Q — a reasonably good choice. Fast-feeling, smooth tempo, not flashy. It suits the club.

The face is a thin, high-strength steel construction — variable thickness across the face, which means the hot zone is wider than you'd expect from a club this price. Cobra calls it E9 face technology, which has been in their lineup long enough to have a real track record. The practical result is that off-center strikes in the upper half of the face — where most amateurs actually make contact with a fairway wood — still produce acceptable ball speed. You won't confuse a heel miss for a pure strike, but you won't be watching the ball die 30 yards short either.

At impact, the sound is a clean, low-pitched click. Not the hollow ping you get from a club that's all face and no substance. There's weight behind the strike, and off the center of the face it feels genuinely fast. When you miss — and you will miss, because that's the game — it tells you honestly. The buzz through the grip on a heel shot isn't punishing, but it's not hiding anything either.

It's not the easiest club to aim, and it rewards a reasonably consistent swing path more than it compensates for a poor one. MyGolfSpy specifically recommended getting a fitting before buying. That's practical advice and not just filler. The adjustability makes the fitting worth doing — there's actual flexibility in this club, not just marketing copy about customization.
