Augusta week is three days away. You'll watch the best players in the world miss from six feet on greens that move faster than good sense, and for about three days you'll talk yourself into a new putter. Most of the time that instinct is expensive and wrong. With the Cobra Supernova, I'm less certain it is.

The Supernova is a fang-style putter — the kind of wingback shape that used to look aggressive in the bag and now just looks considered. Cobra built it using a 3D printed nylon lattice insert that sits in the center of the head, where a traditional putter would put a block of steel or nothing at all. They partnered with HP to develop the structure, which sounds like an odd pairing right up until you understand what the lattice is doing: it pushes mass out toward the perimeter of the head and to the back corners, which raises the MOI and makes the putter genuinely forgiving on mis-hits. The face stays far more consistent than it has any right to be when you catch it a half-inch off-center.

That nylon section also changes the sound at impact. This is not a putter that sounds hollow or plasticky — there's a muted, slightly solid click on center strikes that some players will find settling and others will find a little dull. The feel doesn't vary much across the face, which is the point. If you're used to a blade that tells you every little thing you did wrong, the Supernova is a different conversation.

The face technology comes from SIK, and it's divided into four horizontal sections by three black lines. The geometry is meant to generate topspin earlier in the ball's journey off the face — less skid, more true roll from the first foot of travel. Whether you feel that distinction depends on how fast your greens are and how honest you are about your stroke. On slower turf it probably won't matter. On conditions like Augusta, or anything close to it, forward roll from impact is the difference between a ball that drifts off line and one that holds.

Most reviewers call the Supernova one of the better high-MOI putters in this price range. Chip's take: aye, it is, but it's a specific tool with a firm opinion about who should be using it. The single-bend shaft is built for players who take the club straight back and straight through. If you're an arc player — if you let the toe swing and release through the ball — this putter will fight you. Not dramatically. But you'll feel it in the timing on six-footers when it matters.

At address the alignment is more useful than it looks in product photos. There's a single thin line along the topline paired with two wider lines running along the wings. Together they make it easy to arrive at address and know you're aimed where you think you're aimed. Chip has seen plenty of expensive putters sold on alignment aids that don't actually help you aim. This one does. The face width also reads as larger than it is, which breeds a kind of confidence on short putts that a blade simply can't manufacture.

A bloke I caddied for years ago — a twelve handicap who took his putting more seriously than his driving, which is exactly backwards — once told me the hardest part wasn't the read, it wasn't the speed, it was putting a putter in his hands that he actually trusted. The Supernova earns that trust on technical terms. The high MOI is real. The face technology is real. The alignment works. Whether the fang shape earns it visually, I can't settle for you. Address it once and you'll know in about three seconds.
