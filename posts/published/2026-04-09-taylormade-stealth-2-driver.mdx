Ryder Cup, Augusta, Kapalua — you name the tee box, you'll find a TaylorMade driver. That is not a coincidence and it is not entirely because Rory McIlroy gets paid very well. The Stealth 2 has been on professional bags and amateur bags alike since early 2023, and now that the price has dropped to something a regular golfer can actually consider, it makes sense to take an honest look at what you're buying.

The headline has always been the face. TaylorMade built the Stealth 2's face from 60 layers of carbon fiber — not titanium, not a carbon-titanium composite, but pure carbon from the hitting surface outward. They call it the 60X Carbon Twist Face. It is 44 percent lighter than a comparable titanium face, and that weight difference doesn't disappear; it moves to the perimeter of the 460cc head. More perimeter weight means more stability on off-center hits, which is the only kind of hit most of us actually make.

The head weighs around 196 grams, which sits close to the middle of the driver weight spectrum. Most golfers won't feel that number directly, but the result is a club that doesn't fight your swing tempo. The stock shaft on most builds is a Fujikura Ventus Red — a mid-launch, mid-spin profile that works for a reasonably wide range of swing speeds. If you're swinging below 90 mph you may want to look at the regular flex. If you're north of 105, the Stealth 2 Plus was built for you, and it's worth being honest with yourself about which camp you're in.

The sound is different. Golfers who grew up hitting titanium drivers will notice it on the first swing. It's softer — quieter, almost muted. That either unsettles you or you get used to it within a bucket of balls. The feel question is more interesting. A well-struck Stealth 2 feels clean but not quite hollow. A miss feels like a miss; there's no great disguising of it. Some golfers find that feedback useful. Others prefer the forgiveness of something like Cobra's DARKSPEED line. Neither preference is wrong.

What the internet says about the Stealth 2 tends to land in one of two places: golfers who bought it at launch and loved it, and golfers who tried to return it because the sound startled them. Two years on, those reviews mostly reflect the adjustment period rather than a genuine flaw. A bloke I caddied for last autumn switched from a five-year-old Titleist to the Stealth 2 and dropped four strokes off his handicap over the winter. Not all of that was the driver, but the driver didn't hurt.

Distance claims vary, as they always do. TaylorMade says the Stealth 2 added eight yards over its Stealth predecessor in independent testing. On the course, most golfers report modest but real gains — call it four to six yards with a comparable shaft if they're making contact in the right place. The carbon face expands the effective hitting area slightly, so that gain isn't limited to center-face strikes. That matters for people who are honest about their ball-striking, which is most people once they stop arguing about it.

The Stealth 2 launched at $599. That was a hard number to swallow. At the current price — well below the original retail — it is a different conversation. You are buying a 2023 driver that held its own against the best available at the time, from a brand that takes its engineering seriously, at a price that was unthinkable twelve months after it shipped. Masters week is a reasonable time to buy a driver. The pros playing Augusta right now didn't get their equipment for nothing, and for the rest of us, this is about as sensible a place to start as any.
