Every year around this time, the conversation turns to Augusta. The drives. The second shots. The way the best players in the world shape a high fade into the 11th from 185 yards. That's what the cameras follow. What the cameras miss is what actually separates the leaderboard on Sunday — and it happens on greens that roll somewhere between a bowling alley and a freshly waxed car bonnet.

I've caddied on courses that take putting seriously, and Augusta takes it more seriously than most. When you're watching those greens this week and wondering how anyone makes a putt past 12 feet, the answer isn't talent alone. It's equipment that's matched to how a specific golfer strokes the ball. That's true at Augusta. It's true at your Saturday morning game too.

Which brings me to the Cobra Agera.

Cobra's 3D printed putter line has been out long enough now to have a real reputation, and the Agera is the mallet in that lineup. The face is made using HP's Multi Jet Fusion printing process — a manufacturing method that lets Cobra build a lattice structure into the face material that no traditional milling process can replicate. What that lattice does is change how energy transfers at impact. The vibration pattern is dampened differently than a milled stainless face, which produces a sound that most people describe as softer, more muted. If you've putted with a high-end insert putter and preferred the feel to a hard-faced blade, the Agera is in that territory — but it's not an insert. The face is still rigid. The softness comes from the structure, not from a polymer layer.

The head itself is a larger mallet format with a flared rear and a single clean alignment line at the back. Weight sits around 350 grams, which is standard for this style. The geometry suits golfers who use an arc stroke or straight back straight through — the wider base resists twisting on off-center hits more than a blade would, which means short misses still roll out reasonably true. It is not a forgiving putter in the sense that it hides bad mechanics. It's forgiving in the sense that it doesn't punish a slightly off-center contact as harshly as something narrower would.

At $349 retail, this isn't a budget putter pretending to be something it isn't. The manufacturing cost on Multi Jet Fusion parts is genuinely higher than stamped or milled alternatives. You're paying for tighter tolerances and a face geometry that's repeatable in a way that conventional machining struggles to match at this price point.

What golfers are saying: the feel gets mentioned in nearly every review, and the consensus is consistent — softer than expected, better sound than most mallets in this category. Distance control comes up repeatedly, which is the real test for any putter: can you dial pace on greens of different speeds without fighting the stroke? The Agera earns marks there because the face response is consistent across a wider range of contact points than a blade allows. You don't get penalised for hitting it a quarter-inch off-center. The ball still rolls, still tracks. A few reviews from higher-handicap golfers note that the alignment aid is clean but minimal, which is either a positive or a drawback depending on how much help you want over the ball. If you need an elaborate sight picture to feel settled at address, this might not settle you. If you trust your eyes and want an uncluttered look, it works. Chip agrees with the reviewers who called the sound "muted" — that's accurate, and for a certain kind of golfer, it's exactly what they've been looking for.

The people who might not love it are blade putters. If your stroke is precise, your timing is repeatable, and you've been rolling it well for years, this mallet won't add much. The Agera is built for the golfer who's been inconsistent — someone whose stroke works some days and not others, and who needs a head that forgives the sessions where the timing is slightly off. A bloke I caddied for used to say he could always tell in the first three holes whether his putting was there or not. The days it wasn't, he needed a putter that kept him in the round.
